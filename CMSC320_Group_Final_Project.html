1. Introduction¶

Life satisfaction is a critical measure of well-being that reflects an individual’s overall happiness and fulfillment. In our project, we aim to analyze how various demographic factors, such as age, gender, education level, and ethnicity influence life satisfaction. Using data from Statistics Canada, we will explore trends and correlations in happiness ratings reported on a scale of 1 to 10 by individuals from a variety of socieconomic backgrounds.This dataset was chosen for its comprehensive nature, reputable source, and relevance to our shared interest in understanding societal happiness. It offers a unique opportunity to explore whether certain demographic groups consistently report higher levels of satisfaction and to investigate claims about whether specific generations “had it better.” The insights derived from this analysis could inform policies aimed at improving societal well-being and addressing inequalities.

2. Data Curation¶

Our data, "Life satisfaction by gender and other selected sociodemographic characteristics" is from Statistics Canada, the official national statistics agency of the Government of Canada. We will be using the Age, Location, and Education subsections from this data for our analysis. Since this dataset contains a extra information that is not applicable, we will be cleaning this data by examining if there are any missing values (and resolving them if there are any present), and splitting the data into three separate, examinable DataFrames for each factor.

total_group = life_df_age_groups.loc["Total, 15 years and over"]

# Sample size for groups
n_total = n_age_group = 1

# Function to perform two-tailed Z-test on each category
def perform_z_test(group1, group2, n1, n2):
    z_scores = {}
    p_values = {}
    for rating in group1.index:
        mean_diff = group1[rating] - group2[rating]
        std_diff = np.sqrt((group1[rating]**2 / n1) + (group2[rating]**2 / n2))
        z_score = mean_diff / std_diff
        p_value = stats.norm.sf(abs(z_score)) * 2
        z_scores[rating] = z_score
        p_values[rating] = p_value
    return z_scores, p_values

# Loop through each age group and perform Z-test for each age group to the total average
results = []
for age_group in life_df_age_groups.index:
    if age_group != 'Total, 15 years and over':
        age_group_values = life_df_age_groups.loc[age_group]
        z_scores, p_values = perform_z_test(age_group_values, total_group, n_age_group, n_total)
        results.append((age_group, z_scores, p_values))

for result in results:
    age_group, z_scores, p_values = result
    print(f"\n{age_group}:")
    for rating in z_scores:
        print(f"{rating}: Z-score = {z_scores[rating]:.5f}, p-value = {p_values[rating]:.5f}")

# Location

from scipy.stats import chi2_contingency

# Filter columns for rural vs urban areas and satisfaction ratings
life_df_location = life_df[columns_of_interest_location]
life_df_location.columns = ['Location', 'Rating 0-5', 'Rating 6-7', 'Rating 8-10']

# Filter rows for just urban and rural
filtered_df = life_df_location[life_df_location['Location'].isin(['Urban areas 20', 'Rural areas 20'])]

contingency_table = pd.DataFrame({
    'Urban': filtered_df[filtered_df['Location'] == 'Urban areas 20'].iloc[0, 1:],
    'Rural': filtered_df[filtered_df['Location'] == 'Rural areas 20'].iloc[0, 1:]
})

# Perform the Chi-Squared test
chi2, p, dof, expected = chi2_contingency(contingency_table.T)

# bar chart for data visualization
contingency_table.plot(kind='bar', title="Life Satisfaction Ratings by Location (Chi-Squared Test)", color=['blue', 'orange'])
plt.ylabel("Life Satisfaction Rating (%)")
plt.xticks(rotation=0)
plt.show()

# Output the Chi-Squared statistic and p-value
print(f"Chi-Squared Statistic: {chi2}, p-value: {p}")

Chi-Squared Statistic: 2.222714930786606, p-value: 0.32911189969430366

Conclusion

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference in the distribution of life satisfaction ratings between urban and rural areas.

Alternative Hypothesis (HA): There is a significant difference in the distribution of life satisfaction ratings between urban and rural areas.

Based on the Chi-Squared test results, with a Chi-Squared statistic of 2.22 and a p-value of 0.329, we can conclude that there is no statistically significant difference in life satisfaction ratings between urban and rural areas. The p-value, being higher than the common threshold of 0.05, indicates that we fail to reject the null hypothesis, meaning that the distribution of life satisfaction ratings across the categories (0-5, 6-7, 8-10) is similar for both urban and rural locations. Any differences observed in the data are likely due to random variation rather than a true underlying difference. Therefore, life satisfaction appears to be consistent across these environments, with no strong evidence to suggest that location significantly impacts overall satisfaction levels.

Conclusion

Originally, we hypothesized that there would be a positive correlation between higher education levels and increased life satisfaction. However, after running the ANOVA test on the data, we got a p value of 0.999. This is significantly greater than our alpha value/significance level of 0.05, meaning there is no significant difference between the mean life satisfaction ratings of the 3 eduction levels: No certificate, diploma or degree, Secondary (high) school diploma or equivalency certificate, and Postsecondary certificate, diploma or degree. Although this is a preliminary test, it indicates that education level may not have as significant of an impact on life satisfaction as we originally thought, disproving our hypothesis.
